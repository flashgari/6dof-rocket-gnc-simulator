# Week 2 Disturbance Model

Week 2 introduces the open-loop failure mechanisms that the later GNC controllers must reject. The vehicle is intentionally configured with destabilizing aerodynamic and propulsion disturbances so the uncontrolled response exposes the coupling between attitude dynamics and trajectory.

## Disturbance Sources

The disturbed model includes:

- inertial crosswind
- thrust-axis misalignment
- thrust offset from the center of mass
- aerodynamic drag
- angle-of-attack normal force
- center-of-pressure/center-of-mass moment arm

These are not independent visual effects. They enter the same rigid-body equations:

```text
m v_dot_I = R_BI F_B + [0, 0, -mg]
I omega_dot_B + omega_B x I omega_B = tau_B
```

The trajectory failure is caused by the interaction of these translational and rotational equations.

## Relative Wind And Angle Of Attack

The aerodynamic model first computes relative wind in body coordinates:

```text
v_rel,B = R_IB(q) (v_I - wind_I)
```

The component of `v_rel,B` perpendicular to the body `+z` axis produces angle of attack:

```text
alpha ~= |v_rel,perp| / |v_rel|
```

The normal-force magnitude is modeled with the first-order aerodynamic slope:

```text
qbar = 0.5 rho |v_rel|^2
F_N ~ qbar S C_N_alpha alpha
```

This captures the upper-division aerodynamic dependency: normal force increases with dynamic pressure, reference area, normal-force slope, and angle of attack. The model is simplified, but its scaling is physically meaningful.

## Aerodynamic Moment

The aerodynamic moment is generated by applying the normal force at the center of pressure:

```text
tau_aero = (r_CP - r_CM) x F_N
```

For a passively stable rocket, CP is typically aft of CM so the aerodynamic moment tends to reduce angle of attack. The Week 2 demonstration intentionally places CP above CM in the chosen body-axis convention. That creates a destabilizing normal-force moment so the open-loop case tumbles quickly enough to make the control problem visible.

## Propulsion-Induced Moment

Thrust misalignment changes the force direction, while thrust offset changes the force application point:

```text
F_T,B = T d_T,B
tau_T = r_T x F_T,B
```

Even a small misalignment or millimeter-scale offset can produce a persistent moment because thrust is large relative to the vehicle inertia. In open loop, this moment does not need to be large to matter; any nonzero angular acceleration integrates into angular rate, and angular rate integrates into attitude error.

## Attitude Plotting

The CSV retains unsigned tilt:

```text
tilt = acos(z_body,I dot z_up,I)
```

but this metric folds at `180 deg`. During a tumble, folded tilt can appear to decrease after inverted flight even though the vehicle has not recovered. Therefore the portfolio plots emphasize:

- `unwrapped_pitch_deg`: continuous pitch history through full rotations
- `body_z_z`: vertical component of the body thrust axis
- `lateral_m`: integrated crossrange displacement

These variables distinguish true attitude recovery from a body axis that merely rotated through another revolution.

## Physical Explanation Of Week 2 Plots

### Altitude

Altitude initially increases because:

```text
T cos(theta) > mg + D_z
```

while `theta` is small. As disturbance moments rotate the vehicle, `cos(theta)` decreases and the vertical component of thrust collapses. During near-horizontal flight, thrust contributes primarily to lateral acceleration; during inverted portions of the tumble, thrust can project downward. The altitude peak is therefore not an engine cutoff effect. It is the translational consequence of attitude divergence and thrust-vector projection.

### Unwrapped Pitch History

The unwrapped pitch trace is the best diagnostic of rotational instability. The governing mechanism is:

```text
tau_dist = tau_T + tau_aero
I omega_dot + omega x I omega = tau_dist
theta_dot ~ omega
```

Because there is no feedback torque opposing `tau_dist`, the angular state behaves like an unstable driven rotational mode. The pitch angle runs continuously through inverted flight instead of settling about the commanded vertical attitude.

### Body-Axis Vertical Component

`body_z_z` is:

```text
body_z_z = z_body,I dot z_up,I = cos(theta)
```

It is directly tied to vertical thrust authority. A value of `+1` means thrust is aligned with ascent, `0` means thrust is horizontal, and `-1` means thrust is directed downward. The drop toward negative values shows that the vehicle has lost the ability to convert engine thrust into useful altitude gain.

### Lateral Drift

Lateral drift is the integrated result of horizontal acceleration:

```text
m a_lateral ~= T sin(theta) + F_N,lateral - D_lateral
```

As attitude error grows, the horizontal thrust term becomes large. Wind-relative flow also produces aerodynamic side force. The monotonic growth in `lateral_m` is therefore a coupled aero-propulsive failure signature, not merely wind pushing the rocket sideways.

### Controls Relevance

Week 2 defines the control objective quantitatively. A successful controller must drive the rotational state so that `body_z_z` remains near `+1`, angular rates stay bounded, vertical thrust projection is preserved, and lateral drift remains limited despite the same disturbance moments.
