# Week 6: Actuator Dynamics And Control Authority

## Objective

Week 6 adds finite-bandwidth thrust-vector-control dynamics to the ascent simulation. Earlier TVC cases allocate requested control torque into a gimbal angle and assume the nozzle instantly reaches that angle. That is useful for validating the controller and allocation math, but it hides an important GNC reality: the flight vehicle responds to achieved actuator position, not commanded actuator position.

The Week 6 model adds:

- first-order gimbal response
- maximum gimbal slew rate
- maximum gimbal position
- commanded-vs-achieved gimbal telemetry
- gimbal lag error
- requested torque vs available TVC torque margin
- truth-state and estimated-state actuator-limited LQR comparisons

## Actuator Model

The commanded gimbal vector is limited by the angular envelope:

```text
|delta_cmd| <= delta_max
```

The servo response is modeled as a first-order lag:

```text
delta_dot_cmd = (delta_cmd - delta_act) / tau_servo
```

The achieved rate is then constrained:

```text
|delta_dot_act| <= delta_dot_max
```

and the resulting position is constrained again:

```text
|delta_act| <= delta_max
```

The plant dynamics use the achieved nozzle angle:

```text
tau_TVC,act = r_engine x F(delta_act)
```

This distinction is central. The controller may request the correct stabilizing moment, but a finite actuator can delay, attenuate, or saturate that request before it reaches the rigid-body dynamics.

## Why This Matters Physically

The transverse rotational dynamics can be locally written as:

```text
theta_dot = omega
I omega_dot = tau_TVC + tau_dist
```

The controller is trying to apply a moment that damps angular rate and pulls the thrust axis back toward inertial vertical. If the actuator introduces lag, the applied moment corresponds to an older attitude/rate state. In frequency-domain language, the actuator adds phase lag. In time-domain flight mechanics, the nozzle is late.

Small lag mainly increases overshoot. Larger lag reduces damping, lowers phase margin, and can make an otherwise stable rigid-body controller oscillatory or unstable. If the requested gimbal also reaches slew-rate or position saturation, the controller loses authority exactly when the vehicle needs the largest corrective moment.

That is why Week 6 tracks both trajectory metrics and actuator metrics. A good-looking tilt plot is not enough. The controller must also remain inside the physical moment envelope:

```text
tau_max,TVC ~= L T sin(delta_max)
tau_margin = tau_max,TVC - |tau_requested|
```

Positive margin means the requested moment is feasible. Negative margin would mean the control law is demanding more torque than the engine-gimbal geometry can produce.

## Nominal Results

Truth-state actuator-limited LQR:

```text
Final altitude: 31.42 m
Maximum tilt: 11.09 deg
Maximum lateral drift: 10.67 m
Maximum gimbal lag: 1.50 deg
Rate-limit fraction: 0.0%
```

Estimated-state actuator-limited LQR:

```text
Final altitude: 31.43 m
Maximum tilt: 10.91 deg
Maximum lateral drift: 10.58 m
Maximum gimbal lag: 1.49 deg
Rate-limit fraction: 0.0%
```

The actuator-limited cases remain close to instant LQR TVC, which reaches `31.43 m` final altitude, `10.30 deg` maximum tilt, and `10.73 m` maximum lateral drift. The finite actuator therefore produces visible command-tracking error but not a stability loss in the nominal disturbed case.

## Plot Interpretation

### Tilt Response

The tilt traces test whether actuator lag degrades attitude regulation. The actuator-limited cases stay near the instant-LQR baseline, which means the selected gimbal bandwidth is fast enough relative to the dominant pitch/yaw dynamics. The slight increase in truth-state maximum tilt is the expected cost of delayed corrective moment.

### Lateral Drift

Lateral drift is sensitive to actuator lag because it integrates horizontal acceleration over time:

```text
a_lateral ~= (T/m) sin(theta) + F_N,lateral/m
```

Even short attitude excursions can leave permanent crossrange velocity. The small drift change shows that the actuator-limited controller still keeps thrust-axis error small enough that horizontal impulse remains controlled.

### Commanded vs Achieved Gimbal

The commanded trace is the control allocation request. The achieved trace is the actual nozzle deflection used by the equations of motion. The separation between the traces is actuator tracking error. Since TVC moment is generated by achieved lateral thrust, the achieved gimbal is the physically relevant signal.

### Gimbal Lag Error

The peak lag near `1.50 deg` is a direct measure of phase/amplitude loss in the actuator path. This lag is small relative to the `5 deg` gimbal envelope and does not drive the controller into rate saturation. If this error were larger, it would indicate lower phase margin and likely higher attitude overshoot.

### Torque Authority Margin

Torque margin verifies that the requested control moment stays inside the available TVC authority. This is the key actuator-feasibility check. A controller that stabilizes only by requesting impossible torque is not a flight-relevant controller. Week 6 keeps the required moment within `L T sin(delta_max)` in the nominal actuator-limited case.

## Engineering Takeaway

Week 6 moves the project from actuator allocation to actuator dynamics. The controller is now evaluated against the timing and authority constraints that make real TVC systems difficult: finite bandwidth, finite rate, finite position, and finite moment. The result shows that the LQR TVC design retains practical margin for the modeled nominal actuator, both with truth-state feedback and with sensor-estimated feedback.
